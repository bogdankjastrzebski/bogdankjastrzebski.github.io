# On the Geometry of Confidence in Best-Arm Identification

## Introduction

In the design of sequential analysis algorithms, a frequent tension arises between statistical efficiency and computational tractability. It is often the case that methods admitting simple, analytical forms are chosen for their ease of implementation, even at the cost of being profligate with data. This paper argues for a design philosophy that inverts this approach: we begin by defining the statistically optimal, albeit computationally demanding, model. Only from this principled foundation do we then derive approximations, allowing us to understand precisely what is sacrificed in the pursuit of computational efficiency.

This is particularly salient in modern applications where the cost of sampling—be it in clinical trials, physical simulations, or large-scale A/B testing—dwarfs the cost of computation. In such regimes, it is not merely expedient but necessary to employ methods that extract the maximum possible information from each observation.

---

## I. Problem Formulation

We address the problem of **Best-Arm Identification** within the **Probably Approximately Correct (PAC)** framework.

### Definitions

Let there be a set of $K$ arms, indexed by $k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$. The reward for each arm $k$ is drawn from a distribution $\mathcal{D}_k$ with an unknown mean $\mu_k$, which is assumed to be $\sigma^2$-sub-Gaussian. Our objective is one of minimisation; we seek the arm $a^* = \arg\min_k \mu_k$ possessing the minimal true mean $\mu^*$.

Given a precision $\epsilon > 0$ and a confidence parameter $\delta \in (0,1)$, an algorithm must define a stopping time $T$ and select an arm $\hat{a}$ such that the following guarantee is met:
$$
\begin{equation}
P(\mu_{\hat{a}} - \mu^* \le \epsilon) \ge 1-\delta
\end{equation}
$$
At any time $t$, the history of observations $D_t$ is summarised by the sufficient statistics: the vector of sample means $\bar{x}(t)$ and the vector of sample counts $n(t)$.

---

## II. The Joint Confidence Region

The central object of our analysis is the joint confidence region, $C_t \subset \mathbb{R}^K$. This is a set constructed from the data $D_t$ such that the true vector of means $\mu = (\mu_1, \dots, \mu_K)$ is contained within it with a predefined probability.
$$
\begin{equation}
P(\mu \in C_t) \ge 1-\delta
\end{equation}
$$
The geometry of this region is paramount, as it dictates the statistical efficiency of any subsequent inference. We consider two constructions.

### Method I: The Hyper-Rectangular Region

This construction corresponds to the common approach of combining Bonferroni-corrected, one-dimensional confidence intervals.

**Construction:** For each arm $k$, a confidence interval $[\text{LCB}_k(t), \text{UCB}_k(t)]$ is established. The region $C_t^{\text{rect}}$ is the Cartesian product of these intervals.
$$
\begin{equation}
C_t^{\text{rect}} := \left\{ v \in \mathbb{R}^K \mid \forall k \in \{1,\dots,K\}, v_k \in [\text{LCB}_k(t), \text{UCB}_k(t)] \right\}
\end{equation}
$$
**Properties:** The union bound, upon which the Bonferroni correction is based, is known to be conservative. It provides a guarantee that the confidence level is *at least* $1-\delta$. Consequently, the true probability coverage of this region is typically strictly greater than $1-\delta$.
$$
\begin{equation}
P(\mu \in C_t^{\text{rect}}) \ge 1-\delta
\end{equation}
$$



### Method II: The Elliptical Region

This is the statistically optimal construction, derived from the joint distribution of the sample means.

**Construction:** The region is defined by a quadratic form centred at the vector of sample means. For independent sub-Gaussian variables, high-dimensional concentration inequalities provide a tight bound on the squared Euclidean distance $\|\bar{x}(t) - \mu\|_2^2$. This yields a K-dimensional ellipsoid.
$$
\begin{equation}
C_t^{\text{ellip}} := \left\{ v \in \mathbb{R}^K \mid (\bar{x}(t) - v)^T \Sigma_t^{-1} (\bar{x}(t) - v) \le \mathcal{C}_{K, \delta} \right\}
\end{equation}
$$
where $\Sigma_t$ is the diagonal covariance matrix and $\mathcal{C}_{K, \delta}$ is a constant derived from the relevant concentration inequality or, in the Gaussian case, the Chi-Squared distribution.

**Properties:** This region is constructed to have a coverage of **exactly** $1-\delta$. It is the region of minimal volume that contains the required probability mass.
$$
\begin{equation}
P(\mu \in C_t^{\text{ellip}}) = 1-\delta
\end{equation}
$$
As the elliptical region has an exact confidence of $1-\delta$ while the rectangular region's true confidence is strictly higher, it follows that $C_t^{\text{ellip}} \subset C_t^{\text{rect}}$. The ellipse represents a more precise characterisation of the uncertainty.

## III. Algorithm Design via a Loss Function

To construct a greedy algorithm, we require a loss function that measures progress towards the PAC objective. A natural choice is to quantify the best possible guarantee we can currently make.

For any arm $k$, its **worst-case error**, $\epsilon_{\text{max}}(k,t)$, is the largest its error could be, given any scenario consistent with our confidence region $C_t$.
$$
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_{\text{max}}(k, t) := \max_{\mu \in C_t} \left( \mu_k - \min_{j} \mu_j \right)
\end{equation}
$$
The algorithm should stop when it has found an arm for which this guarantee is sufficiently small. This naturally defines our loss function.

**Loss Function:** The loss at time $t$ is the minimum worst-case error across all arms.
$$
\begin{equation}
L(D_t) := \min_{k} \epsilon_{\text{max}}(k, t)
\end{equation}
$$

### The Algorithm

1.  **Initialize:** Collect an initial batch of samples for each arm.
2.  **Loop (for $t = t_0, t_0+1, \dots$):**
    a. Construct the joint confidence region $C_t$ (ideally, the elliptical one).
    b. For each arm $k$, solve for its worst-case error $\epsilon_{\text{max}}(k, t)$.
    c. Calculate the loss $L(D_t) = \min_k \epsilon_{\text{max}}(k, t)$.
    d. **Check Stopping Rule:** If $L(D_t) \le \epsilon$, terminate.
    e. **Sample:** Else, select a new arm to sample, $a_{t+1}$. A principled greedy choice is the arm that is currently the best candidate: $a_{t+1} = \arg\min_k \epsilon_{\text{max}}(k, t)$.
3.  **Return:** Upon termination at time $T$, return the arm $\hat{a} = \arg\min_k \epsilon_{\text{max}}(k, T)$.

This algorithm is, by construction, guaranteed to satisfy the PAC condition. Its computational demands stem from the repeated solution of the optimisation problem in step 2b.

---

## IV. Connection to the Bayesian Paradigm

This design hierarchy—from the statistically ideal model to its computationally tractable approximations—mirrors the relationship between Bayesian inference and Frequentist methods.

The Bayesian ideal is the full posterior distribution, $P(\mu | D_t)$, which encapsulates all subjective belief about the parameters. From this, one could directly compute the desired probability $P(\mu_k - \mu^* \le \epsilon | D_t)$. The elliptical confidence region can be viewed as a Frequentist analogue to the highest posterior density region.

The PAC guarantee is a less ambitious objective than modelling the full posterior. The "1D rectangular method" is, in turn, a further simplification. By replacing the elliptical region $C_t^{\text{ellip}}$ with the simpler, larger rectangular region $C_t^{\text{rect}}$, the optimisation problem for $\epsilon_{\text{max}}(k, t)$ becomes analytically tractable. This yields the familiar and computationally efficient algorithms. This simplification is not an ad-hoc choice, but a principled approximation of a more statistically precise, albeit computationally demanding, ideal.
